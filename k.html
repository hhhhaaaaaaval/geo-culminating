City: Whitehorse, Yukon

Short Run-down:

Whitehorse, Yukon, Canada's capital and largest city, is a unique blend of natural beauty and fascinating history.

Located on the Yukon River banks and nestled in breathtaking scenery, it is a doorway to unlimited outdoor adventures, including hiking, fishing, and wildlife viewing.


It has a population of around 30,000 and still retains a "small town" feel while offering required amenities and services.

The city experiences a subarctic climate with cold, long winters and warm, short summers.

Deeply rooted in the Klondike Gold Rush history, Whitehorse boasts a vibrant culture that is both rich in First Nations heritage and multicultural diversity.


Key attractions like the SS Klondike National Historic Site and Yukon Wildlife Preserve draw visitors every year, while the economy hums with government services, tourism, mining, and transportation.

Overall, Whitehorse is an incredible destination that captures the essence of the North with its rugged charm and outdoor lifestyle.

Part 2:

-Physical Geography-


About the Area:

The region of Whitehorse, Yukon is strong in presenting a picture of the climatic and geologic processes in carving the land.

All around are evident the excellent record of former glaciers.

The classic U-shaped valleys, the saw-toothed ridges of rock termed moraines, and the large number of lakes are all indisputable marks of the incredible shaping capacity of bygone ice sheets.


The mountains, which define the region, are tectonic in origin, that is, the strong forces of the

Laramide Orogeny.
The Yukon River

remains an outstanding force in the land.

Its constant process of erosion and deposition creates terraces, arcuate courses of meanders, and fertile floodplains.

Whitehorse features a subarctic climate with enormous seasonal changes depending on its location.

They experience extremely cold winters, with mean temperatures close to -19°C, and relatively temperate summers with mean temperatures in the vicinity of 17°C.


They are supported by the region's high latitude (i.e., being well out of reach of the equator), its continental location (i.e., being a great distance from the sea's moderating influence), and the blocking effect of the surrounding mountains.

The mountains also create a rain shadow, which contributes to overall low levels of rainfall.

Cold Arctic air masses usually move into the region, heightening the chill of winter. All in all, it's the combination of this extensive glaciated past history, mountain-building, the river's ongoing action, and subarctic weather that has resulted in a land both beautiful and intimidating.

This interaction of factors heavily impacts the regional ecology and sustainable development of the natural resources of the region.




Part 3:

-Population and Employment-


Population Information:

Whitehorse, the Yukon's largest city and an estimated 30,000 to 35,000 population, is expanding primarily as a result of immigration from within Canada and overseas, drawn by jobs, quality of life, and the appeal of the natural environment, supplemented by natural increase.


Its population, skewed to the youthful end although aging, has a high level of educational attainment, supported by Yukon University and the demands of its service economy, with government, healthcare, education, and retail being large sectors, led in large part by a robust tourism industry and supplemented by resource-based activities like mining and forestry.


The city faces environmental challenges, primarily climate change-related, including thawing permafrost, increased wildfire risk, and altered precipitation levels, along with other sustainability concerns like waste management, energy, housing, and transportation.

To address them, Whitehorse has climate action strategies, promotes the use of renewable energy and energy efficiency, and is in the process of improving waste management, supporting local food systems, and fostering sustainable economic development, in collaboration with First Nations communities on environmental initiatives.




Part 4.1:

-Our Expansion Project-

Our Project:

Whitehorse's planned growth is an integrated master plan to accommodate the territorially located explosively rising population and capitalize on its sheer economic potential.

The underlying assumption is founded upon an acute need for more housing to alleviate the current deficit induced by in-migration prompted by economic realities within the natural resource sector as well as by the appeal of the Yukon's high standard of living and the rise of home work.


This development considers Whitehorse's strategic geographical advantages, such as the possibility of it serving as a so-called "sleeper suburb" that caters to population overpopulation and housing costs in large cities and yet continues to act as a gateway region.

The plan also leverages the Yukon's huge natural resources base, encompassing its mining, forestry, and tourism sectors, to generate new jobs, attract educated labor, and trigger economic growth.



Development will happen through many aspects.


First of all, a broad range of residential areas is zoned, from single-family homes to townhouses, apartments, condos, and specialized housing for individuals with different needs and incomes.

Affordability will be a significant aspect, and policies like inclusionary zoning, government subsidies, density bonuses, and seeking new types of construction will be the key factors.


Green building practices, such as energy-efficient designs and the use of renewable energy, will be embraced as well.

Second, the expansion will encompass industrial areas and parks for light manufacturing, processing of resources, and logistics activities to augment economic diversification and job creation and green industrial output.

Third, commercial spaces like commercial complexes, office buildings, and mixed-use developments will foster entrepreneurship, improve the hospitality and tourism industries, and nurture technology and innovation sectors.


Finally, the expansion involves extensive infrastructure development, encompassing improvement of transportation networks, like roads and public transport improvement, improvement of utilities such as water, wastewater, and energy networks, and the provision of requisite social infrastructure, such as new schools, health centres, and emergency services.

By pursuing this multi-faceted approach, Whitehorse aims to develop a thriving, sustainable community that welcomes newcomers while preserving its unique character and offering a high quality of life for all residents.

Part 4.3:

-Community Expansion-


In planning the development of Whitehorse, sustainability is a basic principle woven into every step of development—residential, industrial, and commercial.

Prioritizing environmentally-friendly technologies and practices enables us to build a sustainable community that not only meets the needs of the current but also preserves resources for the future.

Here's how we plan to integrate sustainability into every aspect of the growth.



Residential Areas


The new residential developments shall be designed with energy efficiency and sustainability in mind.

We shall optimize the use of green building materials and incorporate advanced construction techniques, including passive solar design, energy-efficient insulation, and high-performance windows, to minimize energy consumption and the emissions of greenhouse gases.

We will also encourage the installation of renewable energy sources such as solar panels in residential buildings to allow residents to generate their own electricity.

Purposefully designed green spaces such as community gardens, parks, and tree-lined streets will maintain local biodiversity, provide recreational space for residents, and contribute to a general attractiveness of the neighborhoods.

In addition, we will implement water conservation measures, such as rainwater harvesting systems and drought-resistant landscape, to promote responsible water use.



Industrial Buildings:


Industrial sustainability will be led by the development of eco-friendly buildings that have the main aim of minimizing their environmental impact.

We will promote green building concepts, such as LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design), to ensure that new industrial buildings are resource-efficient throughout their lifespan.

Moreover, we will encourage industries to adopt green operations, such as waste reduction, recycling, and the use of renewable materials in their companies.

Forming partnerships with domestic businesses will make a circular economy model, in which waste products of one industry can be processed as raw material for another.

Also, industrial space infrastructure shall be equipped with electric vehicle charging facilities and encourage use of mass transport by the workers, hence reducing carbon emission caused by travelling.



Business Spaces:


Business spaces will be rendered environmentally friendly with special attention to environmental business practices.

There will be energy-efficient illumination, HVAC and water-saving facilities in new building structures.

We will promote the growth of green businesses, such as organic shops, environmentally friendly service companies, and locally sourced cafes.

Commercial areas will be planned with walkable designs to encourage walking and cycling as means of transportation apart from cars.

By integrating green areas in commercial areas, we can enhance the quality of air and create a pleasant environment for customers and employees.

In addition, we will address the development of community programs, such as farmer's markets and neighborhood art displays in commercial districts, which not only benefit local entrepreneurs but also foster a sense of community participation.



Transportation and Infrastructure:


Sustainability is also practiced in transportation and infrastructure development.

We will invest in mass transit systems to provide affordable and effective alternatives to car travel, thereby decreasing traffic and promoting lower emissions.

Pedestrian paths and cycling lanes will be included in the planning, allowing residents to move around easily and actively in the community.

Green roofs, permeable pavement, and efficient stormwater management practices will be adopted as green infrastructure systems to mitigate urban heat effects and runoff, and render the community climate change resilient.



Summary:


By incorporating sustainable practice into every aspect of the expansion plan, we can create a model community that unites economic growth with environmental stewardship.

This holistic approach to development not only enhances the quality of life for existing residents but also ensures that Whitehorse will remain a vibrant, livable community in the years to come.

Lastly, our commitment to sustainability is a foundation of this growth, a set of principles that shape how we build, live, and interact in our community.

Quick Summary:



Bylaws:


1. Definition:

- Bylaws are locally enforceable rules or regulations of municipal government. They are enacted to govern a particular area and have the force of law behind them.


2. Purpose:

- Bylaws are intended to address a variety of issues of local concern, including zoning, land use, building codes, health and safety, and standards of the community.


3. Process:

- The bylaw-making process typically involves research, public consultation, and approval by City Council. It may involve multiple readings and a public hearing before final adoption.


4. Enforcement:

- Bylaws are enforced by municipal officials. Non-compliance can result in fines or other penalties.


5. Examples in the Context of the Official Community Plan:

- The amendments mentioned (e.g., Bylaw 2024-22) are formal updates to existing bylaws within the community planning process. Adopted, amended, or repealed policies are commonly linked to a specific bylaw.



Policies:


1. Definition:

- Policies are guiding statements or principles that establish how specific matters are to be handled by the municipal government. Policies, as opposed to bylaws, are not typically legally enforceable but more of a decision-making guide.


2. Purpose:

- Policies are intended to provide clarity and direction on a range of topics—road development, nature conservation, housing, and community services—helping to guide the actions of the City Council and city staff.


3. Development Process:

- Policies may be a result of research, public input, and needs assessment of the community. Policies may be added or changed more simply than bylaws because they typically do not have to follow the same formal legislative procedure.


4. Role in Planning:

- In an Official Community Plan (OCP), policies guide how the municipality will make future planning decisions. They provide a strategic direction to land use, development, and resource management.


5. Specific Examples from the OCP:

- New and amended policies like "Policy 15.6.5" or "Policy 7.15" would reflect the evolving objectives of the city on issues like housing density, green space management, or transportation. The precise content of each policy would need to be studied in the context of the OCP to determine its precise implications.



Integration of Policies and Bylaws in the OCP


- The amendments listed (i.e., new, revised, and deleted policies) indicate whether the City Council is actively updating its planning strategy to meet changing conditions, such as population growth, environmental issues, or public comment.

- Policy Amendments such as renumbering certain policies can involve clarifying their intention more precisely or updating them to keep them in line with general community goals. For example, revisions to land use designations on Map 5 are a response to evolving development priorities or necessities, which is recorded by accompanying policy amendments.
